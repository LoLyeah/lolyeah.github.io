Overview

The Archipelagic Fracture

Indonesia represents a geopolitical and demographic anomaly — the world's largest archipelagic state, bound together by a shared colonial history rather than intrinsic geographic, linguistic, or ethnic cohesion.

With over 56% of its population and economic output hyper-concentrated on Java, while the outer islands hold the overwhelming majority of natural resources — coal, oil, gas, nickel, copper — the modern Indonesian state operates on a persistent core-periphery tension.

This analysis constructs a rigorous hypothetical: if Jakarta's central authority collapsed, what entities would emerge, how would they fare, and what would the global consequences be?

Structural Mechanics of Dissolution

How the State Would Fracture

Viability of any emergent state hinges on three pillars: demographic and economic critical mass, localized military capability, and the Water-Energy-Food (WEF) security nexus.

The Localization of the TNI

With approximately 404,500 active personnel, the TNI is uniquely structured around a territorial command system — the Kodam (Regional Military Commands) — spread across all provinces. Upon fracture, these localized Kodams would sever ties with central headquarters in Cilangkap, Jakarta, and form the foundational standing armies of the new island-states.

The naval and air force would fracture along the three Koarmada (Fleet Command) geographic zones. Advanced platforms — including newly acquired Dassault Rafale fighters, F-16s, and Su-27/30 Flankers — would be inherited by whichever entity controls the base they are stationed at.

Java would emerge as the undisputed military, industrial, and demographic hegemon of the fractured archipelago — but paralyzed by severe, existential resource constraints. With over 151 million people and an inherited GDP exceeding $816 billion (56.48% of former national output), Java represents a massive, highly urbanized industrial base.

Java's military inheritance is decisive: the TNI headquarters in Jakarta, the elite Kostrad strategic reserve divisions, the Surabaya naval hub with the Martadinata-class frigates, and the PT PAL state shipyards providing exclusive capacity for naval vessel construction and repair. Critically, PT PAL is the construction site for Indonesia's two confirmed Scorpène Evolved submarines — contracted with France's Naval Group in 2025 and entering domestic construction at Surabaya in June 2026. These next-generation diesel-electric submarines, equipped with lithium-ion batteries, would fall entirely under Javanese control upon fracture, giving Java an asymmetric undersea warfare advantage over every other emergent state in the archipelago. F-16 fleets at Iswahyudi and strategic airlift at Halim Perdanakusuma further cement aerial dominance.

Yet the Java-Bali grid — commanding 80% of former national electricity demand — runs almost entirely on coal shipped from Kalimantan and Sumatra. A severance of this supply would trigger cascading industrial blackouts. This structural energy dependency nearly guarantees an aggressive outward posture: the Javanese Navy would be compelled to coerce resource-producing neighbors into energy supply agreements, making Java the primary destabilizing actor in the fractured archipelago.

Sumatra stands as the most comprehensively viable and balanced independent state in this scenario. Its unparalleled strategic advantage: it forms the western and southern boundaries of both the Strait of Malacca and the Sunda Strait — through which flows the majority of energy shipments bound for China, Japan, and South Korea.

Economically, Sumatra is near self-sufficient: massive petroleum reserves (Riau and Aceh offshore fields), vast coal deposits in South Sumatra, and the global epicenter of palm oil and rubber production provide a diversified export base. Koarmada I, structured explicitly to patrol the Malacca Strait, would constitute a capable naval deterrent, supplemented by Rafale fighters at Roesmin Nurjadin (Pekanbaru) — the most capable air assets inherited by any outer island.

Sumatra's primary vulnerability is internal: the fiercely independent Acehnese north (former GAM territory), the resource-heavy south, and the ethnically distinct Batak and Minangkabau interior represent divergent political centers of gravity. Without an equitable revenue-sharing framework from oil and gas extraction, secondary secessionist movements within Sumatra itself become a real probability.

Kalimantan possesses one of the highest per capita resource endowments in the world — holding the vast majority of the region's coal reserves and significant offshore petroleum and gas — yet it would emerge as the most militarily vulnerable major landmass in the scenario. With only 17.2 million people spread across a massive geographic area, it cannot field sufficient forces to defend its extensive maritime borders or offshore energy platforms.

The recent multi-billion-dollar construction of Nusantara, the new capital city in East Kalimantan, provides modernized administrative infrastructure, but the island is physically shared with Malaysian Sabah and Sarawak and Brunei — creating natural vectors for integration rather than isolation.

Kalimantan would be forced to seek an external protector almost immediately. The most likely outcomes: deep security alliance with Malaysia forming a pan-Borneo economic zone, or inviting permanent Chinese naval and commercial presence to secure coal export routes. Either path trades true sovereignty for physical survival, transforming Kalimantan into a highly contested geopolitical proxy battleground.

Sulawesi's global relevance is tied almost entirely to its massive laterite nickel deposits — among the largest concentrations in Indonesia, which holds the world's largest national nickel reserves. The Morowali Industrial Park has attracted billions in FDI from Chinese state-owned enterprises to supply global EV battery and stainless steel supply chains. This makes Sulawesi a critical mineral node for the global energy transition.

Geographically, Sulawesi is defensible: a rugged, four-pronged star shape with mountainous interiors adjacent to the strategic Makassar Strait — a vital deep-water route between the Indian and Pacific Oceans. It would inherit Su-27/30 fighters at Hasanuddin Air Base, providing some aerial deterrence.

Because its nickel is deemed a critical strategic mineral, Sulawesi would face intense coercive pressure from both Beijing and Washington. China already dominates the island's smelting infrastructure. To protect this supply chain, China would likely offer Sulawesi advanced air defense systems, maritime militia support, and capital injections — effectively absorbing the island into its geoeconomic sphere. Sulawesi would have the least room to maneuver of any Tier II entity.

Papua represents the most volatile and tragic outcome of this scenario. Despite staggering mineral resources — notably the Grasberg complex (one of the world's largest gold and copper deposits) — the region suffers extreme structural vulnerabilities: food insecurity, severe infrastructure deficits, impenetrable jungle topography, and an active armed separatist movement in the Free Papua Movement (OPM) driven by deep ethno-nationalist grievances dating to the contested 1969 "Act of Free Choice."

Papua would not emerge as a unified state. Jakarta's collapse would trigger civil war between TNI units (Kodam Cenderawasih and Kasuari, Koarmada III at Sorong) and mobilized indigenous armed groups. Given the immense strategic value of Grasberg and other concessions, foreign intervention would be almost certain. Multinational mining conglomerates backed by Western governments would likely fund private security or facilitate external military presence to secure extraction zones. The outcome: a mosaic of indigenous tribal territories and heavily fortified corporate enclaves in perpetual low-intensity conflict.

Maluku is a classic case of extreme archipelagic vulnerability. Hundreds of small islands dispersed across the Banda Sea lack the economic mass, unified infrastructure, or tax base to sustain a modern state. The historical Republic of South Moluccas (RMS) separatist movement indicates latent sovereignty aspirations, but these cannot be translated into fiscal reality given a negligible GDP base.

While Maluku would inherit fragments of Koarmada III based in Ambon, the sheer logistical cost of administering such a fragmented maritime territory makes true independence economically unfeasible. Maluku faces a stark binary: devolve into a loose confederation of subsistence-economy micro-states and informal smuggling hubs, or seek integration with a stronger maritime power — most likely a unified Sulawesi to the west, or reliance on Australian maritime patrols to police its vast EEZ against illegal foreign fishing fleets.

Maritime Architecture

Strategic Chokepoints: Shattering the Legal Framework

The Indonesian archipelago sits astride the primary maritime corridors connecting the Indian and Pacific Oceans. UNCLOS currently governs the Archipelagic Sea Lanes (ALKI), permitting relatively unimpeded transit. Balkanization shatters this framework — control over critical chokepoints falls to newly emergent, highly insecure, and potentially hostile states.

Geopolitics

The Proxy War Architecture

The fragmentation of Indonesia transcends regional politics — it represents a fundamental threat to the global economic and military architecture. The mismatch between demographics, industrial capacity, and natural resources guarantees sustained external intervention.

  • Back the Sumatran Federation to keep the Malacca Strait open
  • Secure Western mining interests in Papua (Freeport-McMoRan)
  • Prevent Chinese hegemony over Lombok and Sunda straits
  • Arm sales and intelligence sharing to counter PRC expansion
  • Australia extends humanitarian/security umbrella to Nusa Tenggara

People's Republic of China

The former Indonesian policy of "active alignment" and strategic hedging between superpowers would evaporate entirely. The fractured archipelago would be replaced by a rigid system of patron-client state relationships, transforming Southeast Asia's largest nation into the premier geopolitical battleground of the early 21st century.

Revised Assessment

Conclusions & Critical Reassessment

The original document's analytical framework is largely sound and well-sourced — but its rhetorical certainty around inevitability overstates determinism in a complex scenario. A rigorous reassessment moderates several conclusions.

Original Conclusion

The analysis presents war, Javanese expansionism, and total Chinese absorption of resource states as mechanically "guaranteed" and "inevitable" outcomes. It dismisses the possibility of negotiated settlements, regional institutional frameworks, or domestic political agency in shaping post-fracture trajectories.

The structural interdependencies are real and severe, but the leap from "dependency" to "war" bypasses the agency of political leaders, the deterrent role of ASEAN diplomatic norms, and historical precedents for negotiated dissolution (e.g., the Czech-Slovak peaceful divorce, Sudan-South Sudan partial arrangements).

Revised Assessment

The structural conditions identified — energy dependency, resource asymmetry, demographic imbalance — do create powerful incentives for coercion and conflict, but these are probabilistic drivers, not mechanical certainties. The pace of dissolution, the existence of charismatic leadership, the degree of international monitoring, and ASEAN's institutional response capacity would all significantly modulate outcomes.

More accurate framing: a high-conflict scenario is the most likely outcome under rapid, unmanaged dissolution — but not the only one. A slower political fracture allowing negotiated resource-sharing agreements could produce a looser confederation model, particularly given that Java's industrial base and the outer islands' raw materials are genuinely interdependent rather than zero-sum.

What the Analysis Gets Right

The tiered viability framework is analytically robust. Java as a constrained leviathan, Sumatra as the most comprehensively viable entity (given its balanced resource/demographic/strategic profile), and the assignment of Kalimantan and Sulawesi to Tier II on grounds of demographic thinness are all well-supported by the underlying data. The chokepoint analysis and the identification of China's existing structural leverage over Sulawesi's nickel sector are among the strongest sections.

Key Uncertainties Understated

The analysis underweights several variables: (1) the role of the Acehnese political elite in Sumatra's internal cohesion; (2) the possibility that TNI officers across islands might refuse to sever ties with Java for personal or institutional reasons; (3) the significant fiscal and political cost to China of actually managing resource proxy states at this scale; and (4) the reaction of Indonesia's 240+ million non-Javanese Muslims as a political force that cross-cuts the island-based ethnic divisions the analysis treats as the dominant cleavage.
