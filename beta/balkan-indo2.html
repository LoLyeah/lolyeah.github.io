Overview

The Archipelagic Fracture

Indonesia represents a geopolitical and demographic anomaly — the world's largest archipelagic state, bound together by a shared colonial history rather than intrinsic geographic, linguistic, or ethnic cohesion.

With over 56% of population and economic output concentrated on Java while the outer islands hold the overwhelming majority of natural resources — coal, oil, gas, nickel, copper — the modern Indonesian state operates on a persistent core-periphery tension.

This analysis constructs a rigorous hypothetical: if Jakarta's central authority collapsed, what entities would emerge, how would they fare, and what would the global consequences be? Each emergent entity is assigned an Independent Viability Score (0–100) — an aggregate metric evaluating capacity to survive as a fully sovereign entity without immediate systemic collapse, civil war, or forced annexation.

Structural Mechanics of Dissolution

How the State Would Fracture

Viability of any emergent state hinges on three pillars: demographic and economic critical mass, localized military capability, and the Water-Energy-Food (WEF) security nexus — with SARA-based identity conflicts as the primary accelerant of violence.

⚑ The SARA Catalyst — Ethnicity, Religion, Race & Inter-group Relations

While economic and military fractures outline the physical borders of a balkanized Indonesia, the primary accelerant for violence would be rooted in identity. Indonesia contains over 1,000 ethnic groups, with the Javanese comprising a dominant 40.22% of the population, followed by the Sundanese at 15.51%.

In Indonesia, this immense diversity creates friction conceptualized through the acronym SARA (Suku, Agama, Ras, dan Antar-golongan — Ethnicity, Religion, Race, and Inter-group relations). Suharto's New Order regime ruthlessly suppressed SARA issues to maintain an illusion of national harmony. Following its collapse in 1998, these suppressed tensions violently erupted into massive communal conflicts across Maluku, Poso, and Kalimantan. In a balkanization scenario, the absence of a central security apparatus would trigger a Yugoslavia-style disintegration, with political boundaries violently redrawn along SARA fault lines — turning neighbors into enemies based on religious and ethnic affiliation.

Decades of government-sponsored transmigration also reshaped demographics across outer islands — relocating millions of Javanese, Madurese, and Balinese into Sumatra, Kalimantan, Sulawesi, and Papua — creating a highly combustible settler-indigenous dynamic in each emergent state.

The Historical Blueprint: Echoes of the Republik Indonesia Serikat (RIS)

A modern balkanization would likely mirror Indonesia's only historical experience with federalism: the United States of Indonesia (RIS), which existed from December 1949 to August 1950. The borders of its historical states provide a probable blueprint for how a modern fracture would settle along ethno-linguistic and geographic fault lines.

RIS Historical States — Modern Balkanization Echoes
Negara Indonesia Timur (NIT)

The State of East Indonesia, with its capital in Makassar, encompassed all territories east of Borneo and Java — Sulawesi, Maluku, Bali, and the Lesser Sunda Islands. A reconstituted NIT could combine Sulawesi's nickel wealth, Maluku's fisheries, and Bali's tourism under a unified Makassar-commanded maritime defense umbrella, dramatically raising eastern viability.

Negara Sumatera Timur

The historical State of East Sumatra demonstrated Sumatra's deep internal divisions — driven by ethnic Malay nobility and Chinese merchants fearing domination by other groups. A modern Sumatra could fracture into a conservative Islamic north (Aceh) and a trade-oriented East Sumatra, complicating uniform control of the Malacca Strait.

Java's Internal Fractures

The RIS included Negara Pasundan (Sundanese West Java), Negara Jawa Timur (East Java), and independent Negara Madura. A severe post-balkanization economic depression could reawaken these localized identities, forcing the Javanese Republic to expend massive resources suppressing Sundanese or Madurese secessionist movements within its own borders.

The Localization of the TNI

With approximately 404,500 active personnel, the TNI is uniquely structured around territorial command — the Kodam (Regional Military Commands). The TNI-AD is expanding toward 37 Kodams and up to 750 Territorial Development Battalions by 2029, placing a self-contained military command in almost every province. Upon fracture, Kodams would sever ties with central headquarters and form the foundational armies of the new states.

Sumatra stands as the most comprehensively viable and balanced independent state in this scenario. Its unparalleled strategic advantage: it forms the western and southern boundaries of both the Strait of Malacca and the Sunda Strait — through which flows the majority of energy shipments bound for China, Japan, and South Korea.

Economically, Sumatra holds massive petroleum reserves in Riau and Aceh, vast coal deposits in South Sumatra, and is the global epicenter of palm oil and rubber production. Koarmada I, structured to patrol the Malacca Strait, provides capable naval deterrence, supplemented by Rafale fighters at Roesmin Nurjadin (Pekanbaru) — the most capable air assets inherited by any outer island.

However, its true economic independence is hindered by structural deficits compared to Java — a lack of advanced petrochemical integration, poor fiscal independence from central transfer funds, and reliance on Javanese engineering expertise and financial markets. Without Java's supply chains, monetizing raw materials becomes an immediate crisis.

⚑ SARA Vulnerability: Decades of transmigration have settled millions of Javanese into Sumatra, making native populations ethnic minorities in southern provinces like Lampung. A balkanization collapse would trigger violent SARA conflicts as indigenous Sumatran groups mobilize to reclaim land and political dominance from transmigrant Javanese communities. Furthermore, the historical Negara Sumatera Timur precedent suggests Sumatra could fracture into a conservative Islamic north (Aceh) and a trade-oriented East Sumatra — undermining unified control of the Malacca Strait.

Java would emerge as the undisputed military, industrial, and demographic hegemon of the fractured archipelago, but paralyzed by severe, existential resource constraints. With over 151 million people and an inherited GDP exceeding $816 billion (56.48% of former national output), Java represents a massive, highly urbanized industrial base.

Java's military inheritance is decisive: TNI headquarters in Jakarta, the elite Kostrad strategic reserve divisions, the Surabaya naval hub with Martadinata-class frigates, and PT PAL state shipyards providing exclusive capacity for naval vessel construction. Critically, PT PAL is the construction site for Indonesia's two confirmed Scorpène Evolved submarines — contracted with Naval Group in 2025, with domestic construction beginning June 2026. These diesel-electric, lithium-ion-battery submarines give Java an asymmetric undersea warfare advantage over every other emergent state.

Yet the Java-Bali grid — commanding 80% of former national electricity demand — runs almost entirely on coal shipped from Kalimantan and Sumatra. Severance of this supply would trigger cascading industrial blackouts. This structural energy dependency nearly guarantees aggressive outward posture: the Javanese Navy would be compelled to coerce resource-producing neighbors into supply agreements, making Java the primary destabilizing actor in the fractured archipelago.

⚑ SARA Vulnerability: Even Java is not immune to internal fracture. The historical RIS included Negara Pasundan (Sundanese West Java), Negara Jawa Timur, and an independent Negara Madura. A severe post-balkanization economic depression could reawaken these localized identities, forcing the Javanese Republic to expend massive military and political resources merely suppressing secessionist movements within its own borders before it can project power outward.

Sulawesi presents a unique geopolitical profile that gives it a slight edge over Kalimantan in survivability. It is geographically defensive — shaped like a four-pronged star with rugged mountainous interiors — and sits adjacent to the strategic Makassar Strait. As seen during the RIS era, Makassar served as the natural administrative hub for the broader eastern archipelago.

If Sulawesi successfully spearheads a reconstituted Negara Indonesia Timur (NIT), integrating Maluku, Bali, and Nusa Tenggara, its viability score would skyrocket — creating a formidable eastern maritime bloc capable of meaningful deterrence. Sulawesi's global relevance centres on its massive laterite nickel deposits (the Morowali Industrial Park), which attract billions in Chinese FDI feeding global EV battery supply chains.

Because its nickel is deemed a critical strategic mineral, Sulawesi would face intense coercive pressure from both Beijing and Washington. China already dominates the island's smelting infrastructure. To protect this supply chain from Javanese interference or internal sabotage, China would likely offer advanced air defense systems, maritime militia support, and capital injections — effectively absorbing Sulawesi into its geoeconomic sphere.

⚑ SARA Vulnerability: The sectarian violence in Poso (Central Sulawesi) between Christians and Muslims in the late 1990s and early 2000s demonstrates how quickly social cohesion can collapse when central authority weakens. The presence of massive foreign mining infrastructure and imported labor has already spiked ethnic tensions. Former ex-combatants could easily be drawn back into religious or ethnic violence — potentially destabilizing the very nickel industry that makes Sulawesi strategically valuable.

Kalimantan possesses one of the highest per capita resource endowments in the world — holding the vast majority of the region's coal reserves and significant offshore petroleum and gas — yet would emerge as the most militarily vulnerable major landmass. With only 17.2 million people spread across a massive geographic area, it cannot field sufficient forces to defend its extensive maritime borders or offshore energy platforms.

The multi-billion-dollar construction of Nusantara (the new capital) in East Kalimantan provides modernized administrative infrastructure, but the island is physically shared with Malaysian Sabah and Sarawak and Brunei — creating natural vectors for integration rather than isolation. Kalimantan would likely pursue a deep security alliance with Malaysia forming a pan-Borneo economic zone, or invite permanent Chinese naval presence to secure coal export routes.

⚑ SARA Vulnerability: Decades of transmigration relocated massive numbers of Javanese, Madurese, and Balinese citizens to Kalimantan, drastically altering local demographics. This previously ignited horrific, large-scale ethnic violence between indigenous Dayak populations and Madurese settlers — most notably the Sampit conflict. Without Jakarta's centralized security apparatus, these underlying racial and ethnic fault lines would fracture violently, leaving isolated transmigrant enclaves vulnerable to ethnic cleansing or forced expulsion by mobilized indigenous groups.

Maluku is a classic case of extreme archipelagic vulnerability. Hundreds of islands dispersed across the Banda Sea lack the economic mass, unified infrastructure, or tax base to sustain a modern state.

Maluku is historically synonymous with devastating SARA conflicts — most notably the horrific violence in Ambon and surrounding islands between Christians and Muslims following the fall of Suharto in 1999. These latent ethno-religious divides, combined with the historical Republic of South Moluccas (RMS) separatist movement, would immediately reignite without Jakarta's policing authority. The sheer logistical cost of administering such a fragmented maritime territory while actively suppressing religious warfare makes true independence economically unfeasible.

As modelled by the historical RIS, Maluku's most viable path is integration into an Eastern Confederation led by Sulawesi — leveraging Halmahera's emerging nickel deposits as a negotiating chip for inclusion in the NIT framework.

Papua represents the most volatile and tragic outcome of this scenario. Despite staggering mineral resources (Grasberg: one of the world's largest gold and copper deposits), the region suffers extreme structural vulnerabilities — food insecurity, severe infrastructure deficits, and impenetrable jungle topography.

The conflict here is fundamentally rooted in SARA dimensions: the indigenous Melanesian, predominantly Christian population holds deep-seated racial, cultural, and religious grievances against the Indonesian state. Decades of transmigration brought waves of Javanese and Sulawesi Muslims into the territory, altering the demographic balance and fueling the Free Papua Movement (OPM).

Papua would not emerge as a unified state. Jakarta's collapse would trigger civil war drawn strictly along racial and religious lines — TNI units (Kodam Cenderawasih, Kasuari, Koarmada III Sorong) clashing with indigenous armed groups seeking full self-determination and expulsion of non-Melanesian settlers. Given the immense value of Grasberg, multinational mining conglomerates backed by the US and Australia would likely fund private military contractors to secure extraction zones. The outcome: a mosaic of indigenous tribal territories and heavily fortified corporate enclaves in a state of perpetual, racially charged low-intensity conflict.

Maritime Architecture

Strategic Chokepoints: Shattering the Legal Framework

The Indonesian archipelago sits astride the primary maritime corridors connecting the Indian and Pacific Oceans. UNCLOS currently governs the Archipelagic Sea Lanes (ALKI), permitting relatively unimpeded transit. Balkanization shatters this framework — control over critical chokepoints falls to newly emergent, highly insecure, and potentially hostile states.

Assessment & Reassessment

Conclusions

The hypothetical balkanization of the Indonesian archipelago would not yield a peaceful mosaic of independent island-states. Instead, the structural inequalities embedded within the geography, demography, economy, and ethno-religious makeup of the region dictate a chaotic, violent, and prolonged transition.

The viability of the newly emergent states is entirely dependent on the intersection of demographic critical mass, infrastructure, resource endowment, and the ability to manage SARA-based identity conflicts. The Javanese Republic would emerge as a heavily armed but energy-starved hegemon, forced into aggressive maritime expansionism simply to power its grid and feed its population. The Sumatran Federation possesses the greatest potential for true, balanced sovereignty through its geographic control of the Malacca Strait — provided it can manage its diverse internal ethnic identities. Conversely, resource-heavy but underpopulated regions like Kalimantan and Sulawesi would rapidly lose their functional sovereignty, transforming into highly contested, militarized protectorates of major global powers.

What the Analysis Gets Right

The tiered viability framework is analytically robust. Sumatra as the most comprehensively viable entity (balanced resource/demographic/strategic profile), Java as constrained leviathan, and Kalimantan and Sulawesi in Tier II on grounds of demographic thinness are well-supported by the underlying data. The chokepoint analysis and the identification of China's existing structural leverage over Sulawesi's nickel sector are among the strongest sections. The integration of SARA conflict history — Ambon, Poso, Sampit — provides essential texture absent from purely economic analyses.

Key Uncertainties & Moderating Factors

The analysis presents war and Javanese expansionism as mechanically "inevitable" — this overstates determinism. The structural conditions create powerful incentives for coercion and conflict, but not mechanical certainties. The pace of dissolution, existence of charismatic leadership, degree of international monitoring, and ASEAN's institutional response capacity would all significantly modulate outcomes. A slower political fracture allowing negotiated resource-sharing could produce a looser confederation model. Additionally, the RIS historical precedent is a double-edged argument: the RIS dissolved peacefully within 9 months. A modern dissolution might allow for more managed transitions than the worst-case scenarios imply.
