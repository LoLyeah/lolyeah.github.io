The Radiological Divide:
Indonesia vs. The World

An interactive comparative analysis exploring the critical differences in workforce density, daily scope of work, educational pathways, and compensation for radiologists operating in Indonesia compared to global averages (skewed towards developed healthcare systems).

■ Workforce & Demographics

This section establishes the foundational difference: supply and demand. Indonesia faces a severe shortage of radiologists relative to its population of over 270 million. While developed nations average between 50 to 150 radiologists per million people, Indonesia operates with significantly fewer, driving systemic differences in how the profession operates locally.

Global Average

~90 per 1M population

Standard ratio in developed healthcare systems, allowing for reasonable workloads and deep subspecialization.

Indonesia

~6 per 1M population

A critical shortage. Approximately 1,500-2,000 radiologists serve the entire archipelago, leading to high-volume generic reads.

▲ Scope of Work & Practice Metrics

Due to demographic realities, the daily life of an Indonesian radiologist differs vastly from an international counterpart. Explore the radar chart below to see how factors like subspecialization, raw case volume, and patient interaction compare.

Subspecialization

Global: High. Many read only Neuro, MSK, or Breast imaging.

Indonesia: Low. Most are forced to be generalists (Sp.Rad) to cover all modalities due to staff shortages.

Ultrasound (USG) Duty

Global: Often performed by sonographers; radiologist only reads.

Indonesia: Radiologists physically perform a large percentage of ultrasound examinations themselves.

Multiple Practices

Global: Usually tied to one major hospital or private group.

Indonesia: Very common to practice at 2-3 different hospitals/clinics daily to maximize income and cover regional shortages.

◆ Educational Pathways

The journey to becoming a certified radiologist varies in length and structure. Interact with the tabs below to compare the typical medical education timeline in Indonesia versus the standard global (US/UK influenced) model.

Years 1-4

Pre-Clinical (S.Ked)

Bachelor of Medicine. Classroom and laboratory based medical sciences.

Years 5-6

Clinical Rotations (Co-Ass)

Clinical clerkship in various hospital departments. Graduates as Medical Doctor (dr.).

Year 7

Internship (PTT/Internsip)

Mandatory government service in regional hospitals or primary care centers (Puskesmas).

Years 8-11

Residency (PPDS Radiologi)

4 years of specialized radiology training. Highly competitive. Graduates as Sp.Rad (Spesialis Radiologi).

Optional

Fellowship (Subspecialty)

1-2 years (e.g., Neuroradiology, Interventional). Less common historically but growing in demand.

Years 1-4

Undergraduate Degree

Pre-med bachelor's degree (any major, but usually sciences).

Years 5-8

Medical School (MD/DO)

2 years pre-clinical, 2 years clinical rotations.

Year 9

Transitional Year / Prelim

General clinical internship year (Internal Med, Surgery, etc.) before starting radiology.

Yrs 10-13

Radiology Residency

4 years dedicated Diagnostic Radiology training.

Yrs 14+

Fellowship (Standard)

1-2 years. It is highly standard in the US/UK to complete at least one fellowship.

◈ Compensation & Technology

Financial compensation shows the most dramatic variance. While Indonesian figures appear low in direct USD conversion, they represent top-tier earnings within the local economy (especially when operating across multiple private clinics). Globally, radiology remains one of the highest-compensated medical specialties outright.

Note: Direct USD conversion. Does not account for Purchasing Power Parity (PPP). Indonesian figures represent combined income from multiple practices.

Technological Environment

AI Triage & Integration

Global: Widespread. Indonesia: Concentrated in major tier-1 cities.

Teleradiology Infrastructure
