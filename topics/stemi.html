What is STEMI?

ST-Elevation Myocardial Infarction (STEMI) is a critical cardiac emergency characterized by the sudden, total occlusion of a major coronary artery. This sudden disruption of blood supply deprives myocardial cells of oxygen, triggering rapid, transmural ischemia that evolves into irreversible muscular necrosis within hours.

Immediate reperfusion therapy is mandatory to prevent massive, permanent myocardial loss. Percutaneous Coronary Intervention (PCI) inside a catheterization lab represents the gold-standard pathway, physically restoring arterial patency and dramatically shifting patient survival metrics.

Killip Classification & Clinical Severity

Class I
No Heart Failure

No clinical signs of pulmonary congestion or S3 gallop. Predicts ~6% mortality.

Class II
Mild-Moderate HF

Basilar rales, S3 gallop, or jugular venous distention. Predicts ~17% mortality.

Class III
Pulmonary Edema

Frank pulmonary congestion; widespread rales over >50% of lung fields. Predicts ~38% mortality.

Class IV
Cardiogenic Shock

Severe hypotension (SBP < 90) paired with clinical signs of low peripheral perfusion. Predicts >70% mortality.

Perfusion Boundaries

Myocardial Infarct Core vs. Area at Risk

The spatial division of myocardial ischemia dictates emergency timing. Prompt recanalization aims exclusively to rescue the salvageable ischemic boundaries (Area at Risk) from expanding necrotic waves.

Infarct Core (Subendocardial Wavefront)

Irreversible necrotic damage. Deprived of oxygen, intracellular ATP depletion halts membrane integrity within 20-30 minutes, initiating progressive necrosis from subendocardium outward.

Area at Risk (Salvageable Myocardium)

Ischemic, poorly functioning myocardial margins supported by coronary collateral circulation. Highly viable if blood flow is restored inside the acute intervention window.

Etiology & Risk Profiles

Acute coronary syndromes are classified based on the nature of arterial disruption, dictating emergent antiplatelet loads, angiographic timelines, and long-term preventions.

Myocardial Infarction Types

  • Type 1 MI: Spontaneous plaque rupture Atherothrombotic coronary artery disease. Plaque rupture or erosion triggers local platelet thrombus, causing acute occlusion.
  • Type 2 MI: Supply-Demand Mismatch Myocardial injury secondary to elevated demand or reduced supply. Triggers include profound anemia, severe tachycardia, hypoxemia, or coronary spasm.
  • Type 3 MI: Sudden cardiac death Cardiac death prior to blood biomarkers, typically with symptoms suggestive of myocardial ischemia and new ECG changes.
  • Type 4 & 5 MI: Procedure-related Infarction associated with Percutaneous Coronary Intervention (PCI) or Coronary Artery Bypass Grafting (CABG) surgery.

Risk Factors & Secondary Protection

  • Coronary Atherosclerosis Drivers Chronic dyslipidemia (elevated ApoB/LDL), arterial hypertension, diabetes mellitus, cigarette smoking, and genetic predispositions act as core atheroma accelerators.
  • Ventricular Remodeling Risk Extensive transmural necrosis triggers high local stress on the ventricular wall, causing thinning and mechanical expansion (negative remodeling), leading to chronic heart failure.

Door-to-Balloon Benchmark

Emergent Reperfusion Timeline

Target benchmarks for acute STEMI mechanical reperfusion. The primary PCI clock starts immediately upon First Medical Contact (FMC).

1

FMC & 12-lead ECG (≤ 10 min)

Perform clinical assessment, obtain 12-lead ECG. Identify ST elevations, load Aspirin 324mg chewable.

2

STEMI Alert Activation

Immediately activate the emergency interventional cardiology team and prepare catheterization lab.

3

ED Transit & DAPT Load (≤ 30 min)

Transit patient directly. Load P2Y12 inhibitor (Ticagrelor 180mg or Clopidogrel 600mg) and IV Unfractionated Heparin.

4

Cath Lab Arrival (≤ 60 min)

Direct transport to angiography table. Establish arterial access (radial artery preferred over femoral).

5

Wire Crossing (≤ 90 min)

Cross blocked segment with angioplasty guide wire. Re-establishes basic micro-vascular perfusion.

6

Balloon & Stent Deployment

Balloon angioplasty and Drug-Eluting Stent (DES) deployment to restore optimal TIMI-3 coronary flow.

Primary Reperfusion

Reperfusion Interventions

Standard clinical guidelines dictate reperfusion strategy based on time to PCI and geographical limits.

Window: ≤ 90 - 120 Minutes

Primary PCI (Percutaneous Coronary Intervention)

Preferred gold-standard. Catheterization lab mechanical reperfusion within 90 minutes of FMC (or 120 min if transfer required). Involves diagnostic coronary angiogram, balloon dilation, and Drug-Eluting Stent (DES) insertion.

Procedural Best Practices:

  • ✓ Radial artery access associated with significantly lower access-site bleeding.
  • ✓ Intracoronary thrombus aspiration considered for extremely heavy thrombus burdens.
  • ✓ Dual antiplatelet loading (chewable Aspirin + Ticagrelor or Prasugrel) immediately.
Window: ≤ 30 Minutes (Door-to-Needle)

Emergent Fibrinolysis

Indicated ONLY when PCI cannot be physically performed within 120 minutes of FMC. Dissolves fibrin clot using weight-based Tenecteplase (TNK-tPA) as single IV bolus. Must transfer for routine rescue PCI within 24h.

Fibrinolysis Contraindications Checklist

• Prior hemorrhagic stroke or stroke of unknown etiology at any time.

• Ischemic stroke within the preceding 3 months.

• Active internal bleeding (excluding menses) or bleeding diathesis.

• Suspected aortic dissection or active pericarditis.

• Intracranial neoplasm, structural cerebral vascular lesion (AVM, aneurysm).

• Significant closed-head or facial trauma within preceding 3 months.

• Severe, poorly controlled hypertension (SBP > 180 or DBP > 110 mmHg on presentation).

• Pregnancy or active therapeutic oral anticoagulant use (INR > 2.0).

• Major surgery or serious traumatic injury within the preceding 3 weeks.

• Active peptic ulcer or recent internal bleeding within 2-4 weeks.

• Traumatic or prolonged resuscitation (> 10 minutes) or noncompressible vascular punctures.

Medical Management

Emergency MONA-BASH Protocol

Guideline-directed medical therapy initiated in parallel with mechanical coronary reperfusion. Note critical hemodynamic safety limits.

M

Morphine

Indicated for severe refractory ischemic chest pain. Induces venodilation, lowering preload. **WARNING**: Avoid in RV/inferior STEMI due to profound hypotension risk.

O

Oxygen

Administered only if oxygen saturation ($SaO_2$) falls below 90% or patient exhibits severe respiratory distress. Routine oxygen in normoxic patients triggers coronary vasoconstriction.

N

Nitroglycerin

Sublingual or IV. Induces venodilation to reduce LV preload and wall stress. **CRITICAL**: CONTRAINDICATED in right ventricular (inferior) infarction or if PDE-5 inhibitors used recently.

A

Aspirin

Chewable, non-enteric coated 162-325mg immediately. Irreversibly acetylates COX-1 to block thromboxane A2 aggregation. Crucial mortality reduction.

B

Beta-blockers

Oral beta-blockers (e.g. metoprolol) started within 24h. Lower heart rate and contractility, reducing myocardial oxygen demand. Avoid in cardiogenic shock (Killip Class IV).

A

ACEi / ARNI

Oral ACE inhibitors (e.g. lisinopril) or ARNI (Sacubitril-Valsartan) started within 24h. Halts deleterious chronic ventricular remodeling, preserving ejection fraction.

S

Statin Therapy

Immediate high-intensity statin (Atorvastatin 80mg or Rosuvastatin 40mg). Plaque-stabilizing, anti-inflammatory pleiotropic effects, lowering LDL target < 55 mg/dL.

H

Heparinization

Anticoagulation using Unfractionated Heparin (UFH) or Enoxaparin. Prevents thrombus propagation and maintains coronary patency during coronary intervention.

Investigational & Cardioprotective Therapies

Intracoronary Stem Cell Therapies

Direct infusion of autologous bone marrow-derived mononuclear cells into the infracted coronary artery post-PCI, promoting local angiogenesis and long-term myocardial regeneration.

Targeted Hypothermia in Reperfusion Injury

Focal therapeutic endovascular cooling of the LV prior to coronary balloon inflation to reduce micro-vascular damage and reperfusion-induced oxygen free radical spikes.
