Section 01

Workforce & Demographics

Indonesia faces a severe shortage of radiologists relative to its 275+ million population. While developed nations average 50–150 specialists per million people, Indonesia operates at a fraction of that, driving profound systemic differences in how the profession functions locally.

Key consequence: A single Indonesian radiologist may read the same daily caseload that would be handled by 10–15 specialists in a developed system — often covering all modalities without the luxury of subspecialization.

Scope of Work & Practice Metrics

Due to workforce scarcity, the daily professional life of an Indonesian radiologist differs vastly from an international counterpart. The radar below maps six key practice dimensions on a relative 0–100 scale.

Section 03

Educational Pathways

The journey to becoming a certified radiologist differs in structure, duration, and prerequisite burden. Select a pathway below to compare timelines.

Yr 1–4

Pre-Clinical (S.Ked — Sarjana Kedokteran)

Undergraduate medical sciences: anatomy, physiology, biochemistry, and pathology. Classroom and laboratory-based. Approximately 3.5–4 years depending on university.

Yr 5–6

Clinical Clerkship (Ko-Ass / Koas)

1.5–2 years of clinical rotations across hospital departments. Completed with the UKMPPD national competency exam. Graduates as Medical Doctor (dr.).

Yr 7

Internship — PIDI (Program Internsip Dokter Indonesia)

1-year mandatory government internship at designated hospitals or Puskesmas. Note: distinct from PTT (Pegawai Tidak Tetap), which is a separate, optional rural service obligation and not part of the standard specialist pipeline.

Yr 8–11

Radiology Residency — PPDS Radiologi

4–4.5 years of specialist radiology training (PPDS). Highly competitive entry. Covers all imaging modalities: X-ray, USG, CT, MRI, and nuclear medicine. Graduates as Sp.Rad (Spesialis Radiologi).

Optional

Fellowship / Subspecialty (KIC)

1–2 additional years in Neuroradiology, Interventional Radiology, Breast Imaging, or Nuclear Medicine. Historically uncommon but rapidly growing as subspecialization demand increases in major centers.

Yr 1–4

Undergraduate Degree (Pre-Med)

4-year bachelor's degree in any major (sciences preferred). Must include prerequisites: biology, chemistry, organic chemistry, and physics. MCAT exam required for med school entry.

Yr 5–8

Medical School (MD / DO)

4 years: 2 years pre-clinical sciences + 2 years clinical rotations. USMLE Step 1 and Step 2 board exams taken during this period. Graduates as MD or DO.

Yr 9

Transitional / Preliminary Year (Intern Year)

1 year of general clinical training (Internal Medicine, Surgery, or mixed) required before beginning radiology. Some programs now offer "integrated" pathways that incorporate this year internally.

Yr 10–13

Diagnostic Radiology Residency

4 years of dedicated Diagnostic Radiology (DR) training. Covers all imaging modalities plus nuclear medicine fundamentals. ABR (American Board of Radiology) board exams throughout. Qualifies for independent practice.

Yr 14+

Fellowship (Standard in US Practice)

1–2 years. Unlike Indonesia, completing at least one fellowship is near-standard in the US. Common tracks: Neuroradiology, Interventional Radiology (IR — now its own board), MSK, Body Imaging, Breast Imaging, and Pediatric Radiology.
