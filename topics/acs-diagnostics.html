The ACS Spectrum

Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) refers to a spectrum of clinical conditions compatible with acute myocardial ischemia and/or infarction that are caused by the sudden rupture or erosion of an atheromatous plaque, resulting in varying degrees of coronary artery occlusion.

ACS represents a life-threatening medical emergency. It is clinically divided based on ECG findings and serum biomarkers (cardiac troponins) into three primary categories:

Clinical Classification & Pathology

STEMI
ST-Elevation Myocardial Infarction

Sudden, complete, transmural occlusion of a major coronary artery. Causes acute, full-thickness ischemic necrosis. Demands immediate physical reperfusion (primary PCI or fibrinolytics).

NSTEMI
Non-ST-Elevation Myocardial Infarction

Partial or intermittent coronary artery occlusion. Causes subendocardial ischemia leading to myocardial necrosis (demonstrated by positive cardiac troponin elevation). Immediate reperfusion is not mandatory but guided by risk stratification.

UA
Unstable Angina

Partial coronary stenosis with ischemia but no necrosis (indicated by negative cardiac troponins). Represents an unstable plaque that could progress to myocardial infarction without therapeutic intervention.

Epidemiology & Prevalence

ACS Distribution

NSTEMI 40%

Partial occlusion with cellular necrosis. Represents the largest and growing sub-category of ACS presentations in modern clinics.

STEMI 35%

Complete transmural occlusion. The most acute and time-critical event demanding emergency department mobilization.

Unstable Angina 25%

Myocardial ischemia without tissue death. Critical warning signal of severe atherosclerotic coronary artery disease instability.

Key Performance Metrics

Essential time-critical benchmarks for emergency response teams in suspected acute coronary syndrome.

10 Minutes

Target window to acquire and interpret the initial 12-lead ECG in emergency departments for any patient presenting with suspected ACS chest pain.

90 Minutes

Target Door-to-Balloon (D2B) time for primary Percutaneous Coronary Intervention (PCI) in patients presenting with acute STEMI at a PCI-capable center.

>99%

Diagnostic sensitivity of fifth-generation high-sensitivity cardiac troponin (hs-cTn) assays at identifying cell damage within 3 hours of symptom onset.

12-Lead ECG Analysis

Electrocardiographic Markers

The ECG is the primary diagnostic filter. Restoring myocardial perfusion requires accurate identification of J-point displacements, ST segment trends, and T-wave abnormalities.

ECG Interpretation

The ST Segment & J-Point

The J-Point is the junction where the QRS complex ends and the ST segment begins. ST deviation is measured relative to the preceding TP segment (isoelectric baseline). ST elevation represents transmural injury, whereas ST depression indicates subendocardial ischemia.

1. J-Point: Key reference point to measure elevation or depression. Check at least two contiguous leads.
2. ST Segment Slope: Flat or downsloping ST segments are highly pathological. Upsloping can be normal but has crucial exclusions (e.g., de Winter).
3. T-Wave Changes: Inversions (especially deep, symmetrical) or hyperacute peaks are critical early markers.

Diagnostic ST Thresholds

ACCF/AHA guidelines stipulate strict millivolt limits to differentiate anatomical STEMI from non-specific elevations.

Standard Leads

All leads except V2-V3

≥ 1.0 mm (0.1 mV)

Requires new ST-segment elevation at the J-point in at least two contiguous leads. This applies to high lateral (I, aVL), inferior (II, III, aVF), and lateral precordial (V5-V6) channels.

Precordial Males

Leads V2-V3 in Men

≥ 2.0 mm / 2.5 mm

High normal elevation occurs in healthy precordial leads of males. Diagnostic threshold is ≥ 2.0 mm for men aged ≥ 40 years, and ≥ 2.5 mm for younger men (< 40 years).

Precordial Females

Leads V2-V3 in Women

≥ 1.5 mm (0.15 mV)

Irrespective of patient age, women have smaller normal precordial amplitudes. ST elevation of ≥ 1.5 mm in leads V2-V3 is diagnostic of acute injury in female presentations.

Complete Occlusion Infarction

STEMI Diagnosis

ST-Elevation Myocardial Infarction occurs when a thrombus completely blocks a coronary artery, cutting off blood flow to a major section of the myocardium. If untreated, cellular death is rapid and transmural.

Key Diagnostic Criteria

  • ✓ Clinical Presentation: Persistent, crushing retrosternal chest pain (often radiating to left arm, neck, or jaw), dyspnea, diaphoresis, or anxiety.
  • ✓ ECG Pattern: ST elevation meeting diagnostic criteria in at least 2 anatomically contiguous leads, or a new Left Bundle Branch Block (LBBB) in clinical context.
  • ✓ Cardiac Biomarkers: Rise and/or fall of cardiac troponin values, with at least one value above the 99th percentile upper reference limit. (Reperfusion must not wait for lab results!).

STEMI Emergency Protocols

"Time is Muscle". The primary objective is to physically restore coronary arterial patency within minutes of first medical contact.

Antiplatelet Loading (DAPT):
  • Aspirin: 162-325 mg orally chewed immediately (non-enteric coated).
  • P2Y12 Inhibitor: load immediately with Ticagrelor 180 mg, Prasugrel 60 mg (if no prior stroke/age < 75), or Clopidogrel 600 mg.
Anticoagulation:
  • Unfractionated Heparin (UFH): 60 U/kg IV bolus (max 4000 U), followed by 12 U/kg/h infusion (max 1000 U/h) adjusted to aPTT.
  • Alternatively: Enoxaparin (Lovenox) or Bivalirudin (often preferred in PCI).
Symptom Management (MONA):
  • Oxygen: Supp. O2 only if arterial saturation SpO2 < 90% or patient is in respiratory distress. (Hyperoxia causes vasoconstriction!).
  • Nitroglycerin: 0.4 mg SL every 5 mins (up to 3 doses) for ischemic pain. Caution: strictly avoid in inferior STEMI with Right Ventricular involvement or SBP < 90 mmHg.
  • Morphine: 2-4 mg IV slow push for refractory chest pain. (May slow P2Y12 absorption).
Primary PCI (Preferred Gold Standard):

Indicated if the patient is at a PCI-capable center, or can be transferred with first-medical-contact-to-balloon time ≤ 120 minutes.

Target Timeline: Door-to-Balloon ≤ 90 minutes (non-transfer) or ≤ 120 minutes (transfer).

Fibrinolytic Therapy:

If primary PCI cannot be completed within 120 minutes of first medical contact and there are no absolute contraindications.

Target Timeline: Door-to-Needle ≤ 30 minutes from hospital arrival.

Preferred agent: Weight-adjusted Tenecteplase (TNK-tPA) as a single IV bolus. (Lower bleeding risk than Alteplase).

1. Dual Antiplatelet Therapy (DAPT):

Aspirin 81 mg daily + P2Y12 inhibitor (Ticagrelor 90 mg BID or Prasugrel 10 mg daily) maintained for 12 months post-coronary stent to prevent stent thrombosis.

2. High-Intensity Statin:

Initiated immediately and continued indefinitely. Target LDL-C < 55 mg/dL using Atorvastatin 80 mg or Rosuvastatin 40 mg daily.

3. Beta-Blockers:

Oral beta-blocker (Metoprolol succinate, Carvedilol, or Bisoprolol) initiated within 24 hours of admission if no signs of HF, low-output states, or heart block. Continued for 3+ years.

4. ACE Inhibitor / ARB:

Oral ACE-i (Lisinopril, Ramipril) or ARB initiated within 24 hours in patients with an anterior STEMI, clinical heart failure, or LVEF ≤ 40% to halt adverse cardiac remodeling.

Non-ST-Elevation ACS

NSTEMI & Unstable Angina

In NSTE-ACS, the coronary artery is only partially or transiently obstructed. The clinical differentiation rests entirely on cardiac enzymes: NSTEMI exhibits cellular death indicated by a rise in troponin levels, whereas Unstable Angina shows no biomarkers but exhibits unstable ischemic characteristics.

Ischemic ECG Patterns in NSTE-ACS:

  • ✓ ST-Segment Depression: Horizontal or downsloping ST-segment depression ≥ 0.5 mm in at least two contiguous leads. Highly specific for subendocardial ischemia.
  • ✓ T-Wave Inversion: Symmetrical, deep T-wave inversion ≥ 1 mm in leads with prominent R-waves (R/S ratio > 1). Dynamic changes indicate high stenosis risk.
  • ✓ Transient ST-Elevation: Brief, self-limiting ST elevation resolving inside minutes (typically seen in coronary vasospasm or transient complete thrombosis).

Urgency-Guided Invasive Timelines

Unlike STEMI, NSTE-ACS timing is guided by clinical stability and the GRACE risk category.

Immediate Invasive

Cath Lab Activation < 2 Hours

For patients with unstable signs regardless of enzymes:

  • Refractory, recurrent angina despite optimal medical therapy.
  • Hemodynamic instability, cardiogenic shock, or severe SBP < 90.
  • Life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias (VF / VT).
  • Acute mechanical failure or acute heart failure.
Emergent Mobilization Required
Early Invasive

Coronary Angiography < 24 Hours

For stable patients with high-risk elements:

  • GRACE score > 140.
  • Dynamic, diagnostic ST-segment or T-wave displacements.
  • Temporal rise or fall in cardiac troponin values.
Urgent Direct Transfer
Delayed Invasive

Evaluation inside 24 - 72 Hours

For stable patients with moderate-risk factors:

  • GRACE score between 109 and 140.
  • History of prior CABG, stroke, or PCI.
  • Co-morbidities like Diabetes Mellitus or chronic renal insufficiency.

Critical Diagnostic Exceptions

ST-Elevation Equivalents

These critical ECG patterns represent acute, total coronary artery occlusion without classic ST-segment elevation. Recognizing these "STEMI equivalents" is vital, as they demand emergent cardiac catheterization.

1

de Winter T-Waves

Proximal LAD occlusion with upsloping depression.

2

Wellens Syndrome

Critical LAD stenosis; biphasic/deep T waves.

3

Sgarbossa Criteria

STEMI diagnostic scoring in the presence of LBBB.

4

Posterior Wall MI

Anterior ST depressions hiding posterior elevations.

5

aVR ST Elevation

Left main occlusion or severe 3-vessel disease.

Acute Coronary Pharmacopoeia

Emergency dosing, loading regimens, and absolute contraindications for critical ACS therapies.

Aspirin (ASA)

Antiplatelet (COX Inhibitor)

Loading: 162 - 325 mg chewed immediately (non-enteric).

Maintenance: 81 mg daily orally.

Contraindications: Severe active GI bleeding or anaphylaxis history.

First-line Essential

Ticagrelor (Brilinta)

ADP P2Y12 Receptor Antagonist

Loading: 180 mg orally once.

Maintenance: 90 mg BID orally for ≥ 12 months.

Contraindications: Prior intracranial hemorrhage, active bleeding, severe hepatic impairment.

Preferred P2Y12

Unfractionated Heparin

Antithrombin III Co-factor

Dosing: 60 U/kg IV bolus (max 4000 U), then 12 U/kg/h infusion (max 1000 U/h) targeted to aPTT.

Contraindications: Severe thrombocytopenia (HIT history), active major bleeding, SBP > 180/110.

Reversible Anticoagulant

Tenecteplase (TNK-tPA)

Fibrin-Specific Thrombolytic

Dosing: Single IV bolus over 5 seconds adjusted to weight (e.g. 30-50 mg).

Contraindications: Prior ICH, ischemic stroke within 3 mo, aortic dissection, active bleeding, major trauma < 3 wk.
