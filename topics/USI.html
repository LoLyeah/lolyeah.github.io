Introduction

The Enduring Phantom of
Federalism in the Archipelago

Since the dawn of its modern political history, the Republic of Indonesia has continuously grappled with the profound spatial, demographic, and economic challenges of governing the world's largest archipelagic state. Comprising over 17,000 islands and characterized by immense ethno-linguistic, religious, and geographic diversity, the question of how to optimally distribute power between Jakarta and the vast peripheries remains the central defining theme in Indonesian political science.

While the nation operates strictly as a unitary state — the Negara Kesatuan Republik Indonesia (NKRI) — the concept of a federal system has historically surfaced during periods of profound political transition. The most notable realization was the short-lived Republik Indonesia Serikat (RIS), which existed from December 1949 to August 1950.

"Federalism is frequently analyzed as a potential theoretical remedy for deeply entrenched regional inequalities, the over-centralization of economic and political power, and the complex management of deeply divided societies."

Simulating the resurrection of the RIS in the mid-2020s demands a thorough examination of historical precedents, modern demographic realities, contemporary regional economic capacities, detailed fiscal independence metrics, and the current geopolitical trajectory under President Prabowo Subianto.

Historical Context

The Architecture & Collapse
of the 1949 RIS

1945
August 17, 1945

Declaration of Independence

Indonesia declares independence under Soekarno and Hatta. Dutch colonial forces, backed by Allied troops, attempt to violently reassert control, sparking the National Revolution.

1946
1946–1948

Dutch-Sponsored State Creation

Facing fierce resistance, the Dutch systematically establish puppet constituent states — State of West Borneo, East Indonesia, Pasundan, Madura — designed along ethnic lines to divide and neutralize the independence movement.

1949
December 27, 1949

Birth of the RIS via Hague Agreement

The Dutch-Indonesian Round Table Conference legally births the Republik Indonesia Serikat. The RIS comprised the original Republic of Indonesia alongside 15 Dutch-sponsored constituent states and territories.

1950
April 3, 1950

The Integral Motion (Mohammad Natsir)

Islamic figure Mohammad Natsir introduces the "Integral Motion" in the RIS parliament — a brilliant constitutional pathway for constituent states to voluntarily dissolve and merge back into the original Republic. Recognized as strategic genius by all factions including PM Hatta.

✦
August 17, 1950

Dissolution of the RIS — Rebirth of NKRI

On the fifth anniversary of independence, President Soekarno reads the Charter of Formation and formally announces the dissolution of the RIS. The unitary NKRI is reborn after just 8 months of federation.

The Central Question

Federalism vs NKRI
The Arguments

Protects Regional Plurality

A constitutionally entrenched division of power is theorized to protect pluralism, safeguard regional cultural identities, and prevent the marginalization of peripheral interests by an over-powerful, Java-centric central government.

Sovereign Legislative Rights

Regional governments would possess sovereign rights over local taxation, resource extraction royalties, education, and intrastate commerce — allowing policies tailored to highly specific local realities, resolving Jakarta's unilateral dictation to resource-rich peripheries.

Empowers Civil Society

Advocates assert that federalism would aggressively empower local civil society, drastically increase the accountability of elected officials, and foster localized innovation ecosystems aligned with the Regional Competitiveness Index (IDSD).

Manages Divided Societies

A federal system is theorized as the most effective mechanism for managing deeply divided societies across vast geographical expanses, as practiced in Germany, the United States, and Australia.

Risk 1: Economic

Dislocation & Migration Crisis

Stark structural disparities in resources, infrastructure, and human capital between regions would drive massive destabilizing internal migration. Citizens would flock from Maluku-Papua to Java and Bali, straining local infrastructure and potentially sparking violent nativist clashes.

Risk 2: Governance

Decentralization of Oligarchy

Federalism would not eliminate corruption — it would merely shift the locus of graft from Jakarta to provincial capitals. Regional governments are frequently subject to severe elite capture, where local oligarchs monopolize resource extraction licensing and cause environmental degradation.

Risk 3: Fiscal

Catastrophic Fiscal Imbalance

The vast majority of Indonesian provinces possess Low to Moderate fiscal capacity. Without the redistributive power of a unitary central government, resource-poor states would face immediate sovereign default — unable to pay civil servants or maintain basic healthcare.

Risk 4: Security

Existential Stability Threat

In a multi-ethnic, economically disparate archipelagic state, federalism could trigger devastating racial, religious, and regional animosity. Removing the stabilizing security apparatus of the central government could accelerate state failure in vulnerable regions like Papua.

The NKRI Compromise: Asymmetrical Decentralization

To preempt the necessity of federalism following the fall of the New Order regime in 1998, Indonesia adopted radical "big-bang" decentralization — Otonomi Daerah. This complex system functions practically as "quasi-federalism."

District-Level Devolution

Authority is delegated to kabupaten (district) and municipal (kota) governments rather than the provincial level — deliberately bypassing the creation of powerful regional blocs that could challenge Jakarta's supremacy.

Special Autonomy (Otsus)

Historically conflict-prone regions like Aceh and Papua receive Special Autonomy financial packages — a constitutionally protected juridical status that addresses centrifugal pressures without risking full fragmentation.

DAU Redistribution

The General Allocation Fund (DAU) and Revenue Sharing Fund (DBH) orchestrate massive necessary wealth transfers from industrial centers to the periphery — the bedrock of national survival and infrastructural convergence.

IKN Capital Strategy

The relocation of the national capital to Nusantara (IKN) in East Kalimantan represents the unitary state's most ambitious deconcentration effort — redistributing political gravity without dismantling the constitutional framework.

Strategic Assessment

Why NKRI Outperforms
Federalism

01

The Fiscal Equalization Imperative

The primary, unresolvable flaw of the federal scenario is the extreme horizontal inequality inherent in the Indonesian archipelago. Fiscal data unequivocally demonstrates that only Java, specific plantation corridors in Sumatra, and tourist enclaves like Bali possess the Local Own-Source Revenue (PAD) required to function as sovereign, fiscally solvent federal states. A transition to federalism would allow wealthy states to hoard tax revenues, triggering immediate fiscal insolvency and humanitarian crises in Maluku, Papua, and Nusa Tenggara. Jakarta alone generates a staggering Rp50.7 trillion in PAD, funding 69% of its expenditures independently — whereas all Papua provinces generate almost zero meaningful local tax revenue.

02

Strategic Control of Natural Resources

A transition to federalism would inevitably result in states demanding sovereign control over natural resources — directly contradicting the foundational philosophy of the Indonesian state. Article 33 of the 1945 Constitution mandates that earth, water, and natural riches are controlled by the State for the prosperity of all people. The unitary state empowers the central government to implement aggressive national macro-economic strategies: the 2023 ban on bauxite exports and mandated domestic nickel processing are designed to move Indonesia up the global value chain. A sovereign Sulawesi or Kalimantan might oppose such export bans, paralyzing Indonesia's grand strategy toward advanced economy status by 2045 and its ongoing OECD accession process.

03

Stability and Democratic Integrity

The political risks of federalism drastically outstrip theoretical benefits. Federalism does not cure systemic corruption — it merely relocates it. The rapid decentralization of 2001 clearly demonstrated that without strong central oversight, local administrative elites quickly capture licensing procedures, leading to widespread environmental mismanagement and monopolization of state assets by regional dynasties. The unitary system, enforced by centralized independent institutions like the Corruption Eradication Commission (KPK), remains the most viable check against devolution of the archipelago into corrupt regional fiefdoms.

⚖️

The Unitary State is Not Merely
Political Dogma — It is an Economic Necessity

The simulation of six macro-states provides a clear, data-driven answer: for Indonesia as a whole, the NKRI augmented by aggressive, transparent, and asymmetrical regional autonomy remains a vastly superior and more stable option than a de jure federal state. The staggering HDI disparity — ranging from 85.05 in Jakarta to 60.25 in Central Papua — alongside highly polarized fiscal capacities, dictates that Indonesia cannot survive as a cohesive entity without the powerful, centralized redistribution mechanisms of the NKRI.
