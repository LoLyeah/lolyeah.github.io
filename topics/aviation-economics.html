01 — Decision map

Who actually controls the ticket price?

Ticket prices are the output of three different control rooms. Separating them makes the reform debate more useful—and more accountable.

A

Airline-controlled

Commercial and operational choices made by each carrier.

  • Schedule and aircraft utilization
  • Load factor and revenue management
  • Ancillary fees and service bundles
M

Market-controlled

Inputs airlines can manage, but cannot set alone.

  • Jet fuel and crude oil prices
  • Rupiah–US dollar exchange rate
  • Aircraft leasing and parts availability
P

Policy-controlled

Rules and public charges shaped by government.

  • VAT relief and airport charges
  • Tariff ceilings and floors
  • Competition and airport access

04 — Policy Friction

Four Barriers to Affordability

The high-cost flight economy in Indonesia is not a product of natural geographic barriers, but rather the consequence of four specific structural and regulatory bottlenecks.

The Fuel Supply Monopoly

Aviation fuel (Avtur) comprises up to 40% of an airline's operating cost. While regional hub airports like Singapore Changi and Kuala Lumpur KLIA boast multi-provider ecosystems with price competition, Indonesia remains under a strict monopoly.

  • PT Pertamina Monopoly Pertamina holds exclusive rights to into-plane fueling and storage across major Indonesian airports, locking out commercial competitors.
  • Eastern Logistics Penalty Distribution inefficiencies and high shipping costs for fuel to secondary airports in eastern regions force ticket prices higher.
  • The Competitiveness Gap Airlines cannot negotiate flexible fuel hedging contracts with alternative providers, rendering flights vulnerable to international price spikes.

Tax Relief & Remaining Fiscal Costs

The fiscal burden is layered, but the passenger-side position changed in April 2026. PMK 24/2026 makes the government bear 100% of PPN on the base fare and fuel surcharge for eligible domestic scheduled economy tickets.

CURRENT RELIEF: PASSENGER PPN = Rp 0 on eligible base fare + fuel surcharge
  • 2026 Ticket PPN Relief Under PMK 24/2026, 100% of the PPN due on the base fare and fuel surcharge is government-borne for qualifying domestic scheduled economy flights. Eligibility and implementation dates matter.
  • PPN on Domestic Avtur Airlines pay an additional 11% PPN on fuel purchases for domestic flights, a double-taxation mechanism absent in Malaysia or Singapore.
  • Import Duties on Spare Parts Aviation components, engines, and parts imported for maintenance (MRO) carry customs duties, import PPh, and PPnBM, raising maintenance costs.

Inflexible Price Ceiling Regulations

Indonesia regulates fares through the Tariff Batas Atas (TBA / Price Ceiling) and Tariff Batas Bawah (TBB / Price Floor), formulated by the Ministry of Transportation.

  • Outdated Assumptions The current TBA formulas are anchored in obsolete 2019 macroeconomic baselines (Avtur at Rp 10,000/L; exchange rate at Rp 14,200/USD).
  • Uniform Ceiling Pricing Because macroeconomic input costs have surged, airlines have compressed profit margins and price tickets uniformly at the TBA maximum.
  • Absence of Yield Management Inflexible caps prevent LCCs from selling ultra-cheap off-peak tickets. Instead of offering dynamic discounts, flights maintain static high prices.
  • Rigid Caps vs. Targeted Subsidies Malaysia applies dynamic caps (RM499/RM599) during holidays with direct state subsidies to airlines, whereas Indonesia relies on rigid limits or temporary emergency tax relief (like 60-day PPN DTP) without resolving structural bottlenecks.

Airspace Protections & Oligopoly

While ASEAN member states push for the ASEAN Single Aviation Market (Open Skies), Indonesia adopts a protectionist stance to guard its domestic network.

  • Defensive Airspace Policy Indonesia opens only 5 major gateways (CGK, SUB, DPS, KNO, UPG) to foreign airline traffic rights, blocking access to secondary cities.
  • Coordinated Duopoly Garuda Group and Lion Air Group command over 75% of scheduled flights, raising the risk of collusive price-fixing cartels.
  • Suppressed Competition By restricting cabotage rights (foreign carriers operating domestic legs), the market remains insulated from regional competition.
  • ASEAN Open Skies Disparity While Singapore and Malaysia fully implement ASEAN-SAM (making KUL-SIN the busiest route with 5.6M seats/yr), Indonesia protects domestic routes by opening only 5 airports (CGK, SUB, DPS, KNO, UPG).
05 — Global Reference Models

Global Benchmarks: US & Europe

Analyzing structural parallels and regulatory contrasts. How Indonesia's aviation market differs from the deregulated free-market pricing of the US and the tax-exempt, rail-competed model of Europe.

US vs. Indonesia: Consolidation & Regulation

Both markets are highly consolidated, but their regulatory approaches to consumer pricing represent polar opposites.

Regulatory Aspect United States Model Indonesia Model
Pricing Mechanism Fully deregulated, algorithmic yield-management. Strict price ceiling/floor caps by Ministry of Transportation.
Ceiling & Floor Limits None. Purely dictated by supply & demand. Rigid Tarif Batas Atas (TBA) and Tarif Batas Bawah (TBB).
Structural Tariff Impact Airlines execute extreme price discrimination to fill seats. Outdated TBA formulas force uniform pricing at the ceiling.
Market Power & Consolidation The US transitioned to a 'Big Four' oligopoly (Delta, United, American, Southwest) following post-1978 deregulation and mega-mergers. Similarly, Indonesia's market is dominated by a duopoly (Garuda and Lion Groups command 75%+). However, while US consolidation led to price spikes primarily on non-competitive routes, Indonesia's duopoly has faced legal convictions for price-fixing cartels, keeping tickets uniformly expensive even when fuel costs drop.

Europe: The Three Pillars of Cheap Fares

Domestic and regional flights in Europe are famously cheap (e.g. €15 tickets), driven by a highly supportive operational and intermodal ecosystem.

01
Aggressive Tax Privileges Cross-border and international flights in the EU carry a 0% Value Added Tax (VAT). Furthermore, aviation kerosene is fully exempt from energy taxes, allowing carriers like Ryanair to slash base ticket prices to the minimum.
02
ULCC Point-to-Point Business Model European Ultra Low-Cost Carriers (Ryanair, EasyJet, Wizz Air) maximize asset utilization by using secondary airports (low landing fees), standardizing a single aircraft type (e.g., Boeing 737 only) to cut maintenance/MRO overheads, and aggressive unbundling (charging separately for carry-ons, seats, and boarding).
03
Intermodal High-Speed Rail (HSR) Competition A highly integrated, state-funded high-speed train network directly competes with airlines for travel distances under 1,500 km. This intense intermodal pressure forces airlines to maintain competitive base fares.
