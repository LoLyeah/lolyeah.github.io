01 — Framework

The Sovereign Viability Index

Independence is not merely about GDP. The SVI measures four pillars that determine whether a province can survive as a nation-state when Jakarta's redistribution machinery stops.

KEY THESIS: The metric for a successful independent state is Sovereign Viability — a composite of fiscal autonomy, resource autarky (food & energy security), industrial depth, and strategic control over maritime lines of communication. Jakarta's 16.6% GDP share masks structural fragility; East Java's "Golden Trinity" makes it the premier candidate.
🏦

Fiscal Independence

Measures local own-source revenue (PAD) capacity. Per PMK No. 127/2024, provinces are classified from "Very High" to "Very Low" fiscal capacity. Provinces where central transfers exceed 70% of budget face immediate default risk upon independence.

🌾

Resource Autarky

A sovereign state must secure metabolic requirements. Rice surplus/deficit positions are critical — Riau and East Kalimantan are wealthy but run caloric deficits. Energy self-sufficiency through refining capacity (not just extraction) is rewarded.

⚙️

Trade & Industrial Depth

Inter-island trade is reclassified as international trade in post-Indonesia scenarios. East Java's IDR 209 trillion inter-island surplus becomes export revenue. Diversified economies score higher than pure extraction states vulnerable to commodity shocks.

⚔️

Strategic & Defense Assets

TNI inheritance — Type A air bases, naval fleet commands, and shipyard capabilities — determines immediate deterrence. Control of strategic straits (Malacca, Makassar, Sunda) provides geopolitical leverage and alliance value to external powers.

04 — Strategic Analysis

Key Geopolitical Dynamics

Four structural forces that would reshape the post-Indonesia order within months of dissolution.

💰

Fiscal Cliff

DAU/DAK transfers cease on day one. The majority of Tier 5 provinces operate a "transfer economy" where government spending constitutes 60–70% of local GDP. Papua's road construction costs 10× Java due to terrain — without Jakarta's DAU, infrastructure halts, civil servants go unpaid, and the economy collapses. East Kalimantan and Jakarta could immediately float currencies; Maluku and NTT cannot.

🌾

Food as a Geopolitical Weapon

South Sulawesi and East Java — with significant rice surpluses — could form an "OPEC for Rice," extracting concessions from energy-rich but food-poor states. East Kalimantan's per-capita wealth means nothing without caloric security. Riau would be forced to trade oil for rice with neighboring Sumatra or Thailand.

⚓

The Naval Inheritance

Koarmada II (Surabaya) controls the most advanced frigates and submarines in the TNI fleet. East Java inherits the ability to enforce a maritime blockade over 15+ provinces. PT PAL shipyard ensures naval self-sufficiency. East Kalimantan, despite its oil wealth, would have relatively light air defenses — vulnerable to aerial coercion.

🗺️

Inter-Island Trade Formalization

East Java's IDR 209 trillion inter-island surplus is the "smoking gun" for its sovereignty. In a post-Indonesia world, existing trade flows become international exports. East Java doesn't need new markets — it formalizes existing ones, converting the archipelago's dependence into hard currency revenue.

🛡️

Air Superiority Map

Fighter squadrons are concentrated at Iswahjudi (East Java — F-16/T-50i) and Hasanuddin (South Sulawesi — Su-27/Su-30 Flankers). These two provinces would dominate archipelagic airspace. Kalimantan's petro-wealth without air cover makes it attractive to external powers seeking basing rights.

🏙️

The Jakarta Dilemma

Jakarta's wealth is real but its resilience is illusory. Corporate HQs may relocate to their physical asset jurisdictions. West Java could impose water tariffs (Jatiluhur Dam) or electricity restrictions within weeks. The Singapore comparison is instructive — and Jakarta lacks Singapore's 50-year head start on self-sufficiency.

05 — Conclusion

The Multipolar Archipelago

Dissolution would not produce 38 equal nations. It would produce a regional system of hegemonic powers, vulnerable petro-states, and failed states.

East Java is the unequivocal winner — possessing the strategic autonomy that others lack. Unlike Jakarta, it can feed itself. Unlike East Kalimantan, it can defend itself and manufacture its own goods. It is, in the fullest sense, a complete nation-state in waiting.

East Kalimantan and Riau would emerge as wealthy but strategically anxious petro-states, likely seeking protection from external powers — transforming their resource vulnerability into alliance currency, as the Gulf states did with the United States.

Jakarta would face the most profound identity crisis — transforming from the commander of the archipelago to a vulnerable city-state desperate for resource treaties with the neighbors it once governed.

For the provinces in Tier 5, independence is not an opportunity but a catastrophe. The unitary state acts as a massive redistributive mechanism. Without it, these regions risk becoming failed states reliant on international humanitarian aid.
