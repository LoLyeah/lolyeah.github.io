01 — Corridor atlas

The same mode solves different problems.

Java rewards dense timetables and high seat turnover. Sumatra rewards network reach, freight cross-subsidy, and buses that bridge disconnected rail systems.

02 — Cost anatomy

Two machines, two cost structures.

Rail spreads large fixed infrastructure and rolling-stock costs over very high volume. Buses carry less fixed infrastructure directly, but pay through fuel, tolls, vehicle finance, and road-delay risk.

03 — Island economics

Java is passenger-led. Sumatra is network-gap led.

That shorthand is imperfect, but useful: Java's density supports frequent passenger service; Sumatra's separated rail systems and commodity flows produce a different investment case.

01

Density & frequency

Java can fill more departures per day. That cuts fixed cost per passenger and makes schedule convenience part of the product.

02

Freight & capacity

In southern Sumatra, coal and other freight can anchor railway economics—but passenger trains must share finite paths and infrastructure.

03

Tolls & network reach

Trans-Java and Trans-Sumatra improve bus speed, but every toll kilometre adds a cost that must be recovered across occupied seats.

05 — Policy levers

Affordability is designed, not discovered.

Low fares can come from efficiency, cross-subsidy, or an explicit public purchase of service. The durable answer is usually a transparent mix of all three.

01 / PSO

Pay for the social service

Compensate defined economy services against measurable frequency, capacity, and quality obligations.

02 / BTS

Buy kilometres, not deficits

Competitive gross-cost contracts can pay operators per service-kilometre while government controls routes and fares.

03 / NETWORK

Integrate the transfer

Timetables, terminals, and through-ticketing matter when buses feed trains instead of duplicating them.

04 / PRICING

Target relief precisely

Holiday discounts help temporarily; permanent support should target passengers or services with a documented access gap.
