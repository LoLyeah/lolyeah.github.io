What is a Stroke?

A stroke, or "brain attack," happens when blood flow to a part of the brain is cut off. This deprives brain cells of oxygen, causing them to die. There are two main types:

Ischemic Stroke (87%)

The most common type, caused by a blockage in an artery supplying blood to the brain. This is often due to a blood clot.

Hemorrhagic Stroke (13%)

A less common but more deadly type, caused by a blood vessel that bursts or leaks, bleeding into or around the brain.

Two Problems, Two Solutions: Acute Treatment

Treatment goals are opposite for ischemic and hemorrhagic strokes, which is why rapid, accurate diagnosis is vital.

Goal: Restore Blood Flow (Ischemic)

The focus is on removing the clot and reperfusing the threatened brain tissue (penumbra).

  • ‚úì
    Thrombolysis (t-PA/TNK): "Clot-busting" drugs given intravenously, most effective within 3 - 4.5 hours of symptom onset.
  • ‚úì
    Endovascular Thrombectomy (EVT): A minimally invasive procedure to physically remove the clot from a large artery, possible up to 24 hours in select patients.

Goal: Stop the Bleeding (Hemorrhagic)

The focus is on controlling pressure, stopping the hemorrhage, and preventing re-bleeding.

  • ‚úì
    Blood Pressure Control: Aggressively lowering blood pressure to reduce stress on the ruptured vessel.
  • ‚úì
    Surgical Intervention: Procedures like coiling or clipping to repair a ruptured aneurysm and prevent another bleed.

Preventing a Recurrence

Up to 80% of recurrent strokes are preventable. Long-term management focuses on controlling key risk factors through a combination of lifestyle changes and targeted medications.
