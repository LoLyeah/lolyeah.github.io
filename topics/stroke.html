What is a Stroke?

A stroke is a medical emergency that occurs when the vascular supply of oxygenated blood to a specific neural region is suddenly compromised. Deprived of oxygen and glucose, neural cells within the ischemic territory begin dying in minutes, initiating irreversible functional deficits.

Acute clinical intervention must be initiated immediately. Systemic thrombolysis and endovascular thrombectomy provide prompt arterial recanalization, salvaging compromised tissue and profoundly altering clinical outcomes.

Perfusion Boundaries

Infarct Core vs. Ischemic Penumbra

The spatial division of stroke pathophysiology dictates therapy choices. Reperfusion therapy aims exclusively to rescue the electrically silent but metabolically viable penumbra from expanding necrosis.

Infarct Core (CBF < 10 mL/100g/min)

Irreversible necrosis. Absolute ATP collapse halts cell maintenance within 4-6 minutes. Reperfusion cannot restore functionality here.

Ischemic Penumbra (CBF 10-20 mL/100g/min)

Hypoperfused, functionally silent neural tissue supported by collateral circulation. Salvageable if blood flow is restored before cytotoxic necrosis spreads.

Vascular Etiologies

Etiology & Risk Profiles

Vascular accidents are classified into specific sub-types, which direct patient screening, long-term secondary prevention, and clinical therapies.

Ischemic Classifications (TOAST)

  • Large-artery atherosclerosis Atheromatous plaque rupture inside internal carotid, MCA, or basilar arteries. Triggers thrombosis.
  • Cardioembolism Fibrin-rich thrombi form in heart chambers (due to Atrial Fibrillation, valve replacement) and lodge in cerebral circulation.
  • Small-vessel occlusion (Lacunar Stroke) Lipohyalinosis and microatheroma in deep penetrating arteries supplying basal ganglia, thalamus, or internal capsule.
  • Cryptogenic / Other etiology Accidents linked to dissections, thrombophilia (APLA, Factor V Leiden), patent foramen ovale (PFO), or unexplained.

Hemorrhagic Classifications

  • Intracerebral Hemorrhage (ICH) Rupture of deep microaneurysms (Charcot-Bouchard) linked to chronic hypertension, or lobar hemorrhage due to Cerebral Amyloid Angiopathy (CAA) in elderly patients.
  • Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (SAH) Rupture of arterial saccular (berry) aneurysms in the Circle of Willis, spilling blood directly into the cerebrospinal fluid space. Triggers vasospasm.

Ischemic Reperfusion

Ischemic Reperfusion Interventions

Reperfusion therapies are dictated by the time window from Last Known Well (LKW) and vascular territory.

Window: < 4.5 Hours

IV Thrombolysis (Tenecteplase vs. Alteplase)

Enzymatic activation of plasminogen to plasmin, dissolving the occluding fibrin clot. Modern guidelines prefer Tenecteplase over standard Alteplase.

Tenecteplase (TNK-tPA)

Dosage: 0.25 mg/kg (max 25 mg) delivered as a single rapid IV bolus over 5-10s. Higher fibrin specificity and less bleeding.

Alteplase (rt-PA)

Dosage: 0.9 mg/kg (max 90 mg) as a 10% bolus over 1 minute, with the remaining 90% infused over 60 minutes.

Window: < 24 Hours

Mechanical Thrombectomy

Mechanical clot retrieval inside catheterization labs. Stent-retrievers or large aspiration catheters physical remove large vessel occlusions (LVO) in patients with salvageable tissue.

Eligibility Criteria (DAWN / DEFUSE-3):
  • ✓ Proximal large-vessel occlusion (ICA or proximal MCA/M1 branch).
  • ✓ CT perfusion or MR mismatch demonstrating small necrotic core and large penumbra.
  • ✓ Pre-stroke independence (mRS score 0-1).

Thrombolysis Contraindications Checklist

• Active internal bleeding or bleeding diathesis.

• Intracranial hemorrhage confirmed on initial head CT.

• Severe head trauma, intracranial/spinal surgery, or prior stroke within 3 months.

• Intracranial neoplasm, arteriovenous malformation, or aneurysm.

• Severe uncontrolled hypertension (SBP > 185 or DBP > 110 despite treatment).

• Minor or rapidly improving deficits (NIHSS < 4 with isolated sensory symptoms).

• Pregnancy or active menstruation.

• Major surgery or serious trauma within 14 days.

• Recent gastrointestinal or urinary tract hemorrhage within 21 days.

• Coagulation abnormalities: Platelet count < 100,000, INR > 1.7, or active use of NOACs.

Hemorrhagic Emergency

Hemorrhagic Stroke Management

Hemorrhagic emergencies focus on controlling pressure, reversing coagulopathies, and preventing mass expansion.

Blood Pressure Control

Immediate and aggressive reduction of systolic blood pressure to a target SBP of 140 mmHg (typically using continuous infusions of nicardipine or clevidipine) to limit hematoma growth.

Anticoagulant Reversal

Immediate administration of reversal agents based on prior medication: Idarucizumab for Dabigatran, Andexanet Alfa for Apixaban/Rivaroxaban, or Prothrombin Complex Concentrate (PCC) for Warfarin.

Surgical Intervention

Placement of external ventricular drains (EVD) for hydrocephalus, clipping/coiling for aneurysmal SAH, or decompressive hemicraniectomy for massive edema and threat of herniation.

Investigational & Neuroprotective Therapies

Intravenous Neuroprotection (Nerinetide)

Synthetic peptide designed to disrupt PSD-95 protein signaling, protecting cortical neurons from lethal glutamate excitotoxicity during transit before reperfusion is achieved.

Targeted Hypothermia & Stem Cell Regeneration

Focal endovascular cooling of the ischemic hemisphere to reduce metabolic demand, and mesenchymal stem cell (MSC) therapy to induce angiogenesis and endogenous neural regeneration.

Breakthrough Technologies

Robotics, VR, & Paired Vagus Nerve Stimulation

Modern neurological rehabilitation leverages high-frequency repitition to force neuroplastic rewiring. Robotic exoskeletons aid in gait retraining, while immersive Virtual Reality (VR) gamifies upper-limb movement. Furthermore, FDA-approved paired **Vagus Nerve Stimulation (VNS)** actively stimulates norepinephrine and acetylcholine release during clinical exercises, doubling recovery speeds.
